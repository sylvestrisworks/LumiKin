# Game Rating Rubric Framework
## "PlaySmart" Scoring Methodology — v0.1 Draft

---

## Design philosophy

This rubric rates games, not gamers. It asks: "What did the designers build, and how does it interact with a developing brain?" It leads with the good, acknowledges the risks, and gives parents an actionable time recommendation. It is not anti-gaming. It is pro-informed-gaming.

Every game gets two profiles: a **Benefits Profile** (what your child develops) and a **Risk Profile** (what to watch out for). These are independent — a game can score high on both. The time recommendation is derived from the interaction between the two.

---

## PART A: BENEFITS PROFILE

### Category B1: Cognitive development (max 50 points)

Each item scored 0–5 where 0 = not present, 1 = minimal, 3 = moderate, 5 = core mechanic.

| # | Skill dimension | What to assess | Example (high score) | Example (low score) |
|---|----------------|----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| B1.1 | Problem solving | Does the game present novel problems that require reasoning, experimentation, or logic? Are solutions non-obvious? | Portal 2 (5), Zelda: TotK (5) | Cookie Clicker (0) |
| B1.2 | Spatial awareness | Does the game require mental rotation, 3D navigation, map reading, or spatial planning? | Minecraft (5), Tetris (5) | Candy Crush (1) |
| B1.3 | Strategic thinking | Does the game require planning ahead, resource management, or evaluating trade-offs? | Civilization VI (5), Chess.com (5) | Temple Run (0) |
| B1.4 | Critical thinking | Does the game require evaluating information, questioning assumptions, or making evidence-based decisions? | Return of the Obra Dinn (5), Papers Please (5) | Subway Surfers (0) |
| B1.5 | Memory and attention | Does the game train working memory, sustained attention, or pattern recognition? | Brain Age (4), Lumosity (3) | Idle games (0) |
| B1.6 | Creativity and expression | Does the game provide open-ended tools for building, designing, composing, or storytelling? | Minecraft Creative (5), Mario Maker (5) | Call of Duty MP (1) |
| B1.7 | Reading and language | Does the game develop vocabulary, reading comprehension, or narrative understanding? | Disco Elysium (5), Ace Attorney (4) | Fruit Ninja (0) |
| B1.8 | Math and systems thinking | Does the game involve numerical reasoning, economic systems, or statistical thinking? | Kerbal Space Program (5), Factorio (5) | Flappy Bird (0) |
| B1.9 | Learning transfer | Does the game teach knowledge or skills that apply outside the game? (history, science, geography, coding, music) | Assassin's Creed Discovery Tour (5), Scratch (5) | Generic match-3 (0) |
| B1.10 | Adaptive challenge | Does difficulty scale with the player's skill, keeping them in a flow state? Or is difficulty used to push spending? | Celeste (5), Hades (4) | Games with pay-to-win difficulty spikes (0) |

**Scoring:** Sum of B1.1–B1.10. Max 50. Reported as percentage.

---

### Category B2: Social and emotional development (max 30 points)

| # | Skill dimension | What to assess | Example (high) | Example (low) |
|---|----------------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| B2.1 | Teamwork and cooperation | Does the game require genuine collaboration where players depend on each other's contributions? | It Takes Two (5), Overcooked (5) | Solo games with bolted-on leaderboards (0) |
| B2.2 | Communication skills | Does the game require or encourage meaningful communication between players? | Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes (5), Sea of Thieves (4) | Games with only emote-based comms (1) |
| B2.3 | Empathy and perspective-taking | Does the game help the player understand other viewpoints, cultures, or emotional experiences? | Celeste (4), Life is Strange (4) | Generic shooters (0) |
| B2.4 | Emotional regulation | Does the game teach persistence, managing frustration, or coping with loss? (Not through punishment but through design) | Dark Souls (4), Getting Over It (3) | Games that monetize frustration (0) |
| B2.5 | Ethical reasoning | Does the game present moral dilemmas, consequences for choices, or opportunities to consider fairness? | Undertale (5), The Walking Dead (4) | Games with no meaningful choices (0) |
| B2.6 | Positive social interaction | Does the multiplayer environment foster positive interaction? Is there moderation? Are there prosocial incentives? | Animal Crossing (4), Stardew Valley co-op (4) | Unmoderated open chat with strangers (0) |

**Scoring:** Sum of B2.1–B2.6. Max 30. Reported as percentage.

---

### Category B3: Physical and motor development (max 20 points)

| # | Skill dimension | What to assess | Example (high) | Example (low) |
|---|----------------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| B3.1 | Hand-eye coordination | Does the game require precise timing, aiming, or coordination between visual input and motor response? | Osu! (5), Beat Saber (5) | Turn-based card games (1) |
| B3.2 | Fine motor skills | Does the game require precise small-muscle movements, dexterity, or touch precision? | Surgeon Simulator (4), drawing games (4) | Idle/auto-play games (0) |
| B3.3 | Reaction time | Does the game train quick reflexes and rapid decision-making? | Rocket League (5), rhythm games (5) | Puzzle games with no time pressure (0) |
| B3.4 | Physical activity | Does the game involve whole-body movement? (VR, motion controls, AR) | Ring Fit Adventure (5), Just Dance (5), Pokémon GO (4) | Standard seated gaming (0) |

**Scoring:** Sum of B3.1–B3.4. Max 20. Reported as percentage.

## PART B: RISK PROFILE

Built on the SHARP-G framework (Saini & Hodgins, 2024) with extensions for monetization transparency and child-specific concerns.

### Category R1: Dopamine manipulation design (max 30 points)

Each item scored 0–3 where 0 = not present, 1 = mild/optional, 2 = significant, 3 = core mechanic.

| # | Risk factor | 0 (none) | 1 (mild) | 2 (significant) | 3 (core mechanic) |
|---|------------|----------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| R1.1 | Variable ratio rewards | No random rewards | Occasional random drops, not central | Loot tables central to progression | Gacha/loot box as primary reward loop |
| R1.2 | Streak mechanics | No streaks or daily login rewards | Daily bonus exists, no penalty for missing | Missing a day loses progress or multiplier | Multi-day streak with escalating loss for breaking |
| R1.3 | Loss aversion triggers | No punishment for absence | Mild reminders to return | Resources decay or opponents advance while away | Active penalties for not playing (base raided, rank decays) |
| R1.4 | FOMO / time-limited events | No time-limited content | Seasonal events with cosmetic-only rewards | Time-limited items that affect gameplay | Exclusive content that disappears, encouraging constant engagement |
| R1.5 | Artificial stopping barriers | Game has natural pause points | Some "one more round" design | Energy/lives system that can be bypassed with money | Hard stop that creates frustration and spending pressure |
| R1.6 | Notification and re-engagement | No push notifications | Optional notifications | Aggressive notifications ("Your crops are dying!") | Notifications designed to create anxiety about missing out |
| R1.7 | Near-miss mechanics | No near-miss design | Occasional "almost won" feedback | Regular near-miss feedback amplified with effects | Systematic near-miss design mimicking gambling psychology |
| R1.8 | Infinite scroll / endless play | Game has clear endpoints | Long sessions possible but natural breaks exist | Designed for extended sessions with no stopping cues | Auto-play, infinite content feed, no natural endpoint |
| R1.9 | Escalating commitment | Game is satisfying at any point | Mild sense of investment over time | Significant sunk-cost psychology ("I've invested too much to stop") | Extreme time/money invested creates lock-in |
| R1.10 | Variable reward frequency | Consistent, predictable rewards | Mostly predictable with some variation | Deliberately unpredictable reward timing | Precisely calibrated variable schedule (slot machine pattern) |

**Scoring:** Sum of R1.1–R1.10. Max 30.

---

### Category R2: Monetization pressure (max 24 points)

| # | Risk factor | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|------------|---|---|---|---|
| R2.1 | Spending ceiling | One-time purchase or free with no IAP | IAP exists but capped or cosmetic-only | Significant IAP with soft ceiling | Unlimited spending possible (no cap) |
| R2.2 | Pay-to-win mechanics | No advantage from spending | Minor convenience from spending | Meaningful gameplay advantage from spending | Progression essentially requires spending |
| R2.3 | Currency obfuscation | Prices shown in real currency | One layer of virtual currency with clear conversion | Multiple virtual currencies obscuring real cost | Complex currency chains making cost comparison impossible |
| R2.4 | Spending prompts | No purchase prompts during gameplay | Occasional shop reminders | Regular prompts at frustration points | Aggressive prompts, "special offers" triggered by failure |
| R2.5 | Child-targeting design | No child-targeted monetization | Monetization present but not child-specific | Colorful, exciting purchase UI targeting younger users | Deliberate child-focused spending design (cartoon purchase buttons, reward animations for buying) |
| R2.6 | Ad pressure | No ads | Optional reward-based ads only | Regular interstitial ads | Aggressive, unskippable, or deceptive ads |
| R2.7 | Subscription pressure | No subscription model | Optional subscription with clear benefits | Subscription that locks previously available features | Subscription with auto-renewal and difficult cancellation |
| R2.8 | Social spending pressure | No social spending mechanics | Others' purchases visible but not emphasized | Social status tied to purchased items | Gifting, social comparison, or peer pressure to spend |

**Scoring:** Sum of R2.1–R2.8. Max 24.

---

### Category R3: Social and emotional risk (max 18 points)

| # | Risk factor | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|------------|---|---|---|---|
| R3.1 | Social obligation | No social obligations | Optional social features | Group expectations create pressure to play (guild events, team commitments) | Strong social punishment for not participating (kicked from guild, let team down) |
| R3.2 | Competitive toxicity | No competitive element or well-moderated | Competitive but with good sportsmanship design | Competitive with rank anxiety and limited moderation | Toxic competitive environment with rank loss and public shaming |
| R3.3 | Stranger interaction risk | No stranger interaction | Moderated stranger interaction, text-only | Voice chat with strangers, limited moderation | Unmoderated open communication with unknown adults |
| R3.4 | Social comparison | No comparison mechanics | Basic profiles without comparison | Prominent leaderboards, rank displays, spending-visible cosmetics | Designed to showcase spending and create envy |
| R3.5 | Identity and self-worth | No identity mechanics | Optional avatar/profile | Identity strongly tied to in-game achievements and items | Self-worth explicitly linked to game performance and possessions |
| R3.6 | Privacy risk | No data collection beyond basics | Standard analytics | Extensive behavioral tracking | Data collected on minors without transparent consent, sold to third parties |

**Scoring:** Sum of R3.1–R3.6. Max 18.

---

### Category R4: Content risk (max 15 points — aligns with existing ESRB/PEGI)

| # | Risk factor | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|------------|---|---|---|---|
| R4.1 | Violence level | None | Mild cartoon violence | Moderate realistic violence | Graphic, realistic violence |
| R4.2 | Sexual content | None | Mild suggestive content | Moderate sexual themes | Explicit sexual content |
| R4.3 | Language | None | Mild language | Moderate profanity | Extreme/frequent profanity |
| R4.4 | Substance references | None | Mild references | Use depicted without consequence | Use depicted positively or in detail |
| R4.5 | Fear and horror | None | Mild tension | Moderate scares or disturbing imagery | Intense horror, potentially traumatizing content |

**Scoring:** Sum of R4.1–R4.5. Max 15.

## PART C: REPRESENTATION & IDEOLOGY (display only — does not affect time recommendation)

These dimensions give parents context about the world the game presents. They are informational, not punitive. A game can score low on representation and still be recommended; a game with propaganda content gets a flag, not a lower time limit.

### REP: Representation (0–3 per dimension, higher = better)

| # | Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|-----------|---|---|---|---|
| REP.1 | Gender balance | All playable/main characters one gender, or heavily stereotyped portrayals | Mostly one gender; token representation | Some diversity; secondary characters varied | Authentic diverse gender representation; non-stereotyped roles |
| REP.2 | Ethnic & cultural diversity | Monoculture / whitewashed / ethnically stereotyped | Minimal or token diversity | Moderate diversity; some cultures represented authentically | Rich, authentic representation of multiple ethnicities/cultures |

**Scoring note:** Score what the game ships with by default, not fan mods. Historical games set in homogeneous contexts can score 1–2 without penalty — context matters.

### PROP: Propaganda / Ideological content (0–3, lower = more neutral)

| Score | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 0 | Neutral — no discernible ideological framing | Most puzzle, sports, and sandbox games |
| 1 | Mild cultural perspective — common in historical or nationally-developed games; unlikely to concern most parents | Historical games depicting one nation's view, games with patriotic themes |
| 2 | Notable ideological framing — parents should be aware; game presents a clear political, religious, or nationalist lens | Games glorifying a specific regime, strong religious messaging, heavy nationalist narrative |
| 3 | Heavy propaganda — game is primarily a vehicle for ideology; content is one-sided, factually distorted, or designed to shape political views | State-sponsored propaganda games, games designed to radicalize |

**propagandaNotes:** If score ≥ 1, include a brief note: what type of content and where it appears (e.g., "Nationalist framing in WWII campaign — Japanese/German forces dehumanized").

## PART D: PRACTICAL INFORMATION

These are not scored on a scale — they are factual fields captured by the reviewer.

| Field | Data type | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| C1. Base price | Currency | $69.99 / Free / €39.99 |
| C2. Estimated monthly cost if engaging with monetization | Currency range | $0 / $5–15 / $30–100+ |
| C3. Total content behind paywall | Percentage or description | 0% / ~20% cosmetic / ~60% gameplay content |
| C4. Minimum session to make progress | Minutes | 5 min / 20 min / 45+ min |
| C5. Natural stopping points | Yes/No + description | Yes — between shrines/levels. No — endless runner design |
| C6. Penalizes breaks | Yes/No + description | No / Yes — stamina decays, dailies missed |
| C7. Requires internet | Always / Sometimes / Never | Always online |
| C8. Chat with strangers possible | Yes/No + moderation level | Yes — unmoderated voice chat |
| C9. Account required with personal data | Yes/No + what data | Yes — email, age, location |
| C10. Platform | Multi-select | PC, Switch, PS5, iOS, Android |
| C11. Accessibility features | Checklist | Subtitles, colorblind mode, remappable controls, difficulty options |
| C12. Content update frequency | Description | Monthly updates, weekly events, static game |

## PART E: TIME RECOMMENDATION ENGINE

The time recommendation is the headline output. It is generated from a formula that weighs risk factors against benefits and considers the child's age.

### Step 1: Calculate Risk Intensity Score (RIS)

```
RIS = (R1 weighted × 0.45) + (R2 weighted × 0.30) + (R3 weighted × 0.25)
```

Where:
- R1 weighted = R1 raw score / 30 (dopamine manipulation, normalized to 0–1)
- R2 weighted = R2 raw score / 24 (monetization pressure, normalized to 0–1)
- R3 weighted = R3 raw score / 18 (social/emotional risk, normalized to 0–1)

R4 (content risk) does NOT feed into the time recommendation — it's a separate parental judgment about content appropriateness.

**RIS ranges from 0.00 to 1.00.**

### Step 2: Calculate Benefit Density Score (BDS)

```
BDS = (B1 weighted × 0.50) + (B2 weighted × 0.30) + (B3 weighted × 0.20)
```

Where:
- B1 weighted = B1 raw score / 50 (cognitive, normalized to 0–1)
- B2 weighted = B2 raw score / 30 (social-emotional, normalized to 0–1)
- B3 weighted = B3 raw score / 20 (physical-motor, normalized to 0–1)

**BDS ranges from 0.00 to 1.00.**

### Step 3: Derive Base Time Recommendation

The time recommendation is a function of risk intensity, modulated by benefit density. High benefits can extend the recommendation; high risk pulls it down.

| RIS range | Base recommendation | Reasoning |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| 0.00–0.15 | Up to 120 min | Minimal manipulation design. Self-directed play |
| 0.16–0.30 | Up to 90 min | Low manipulation. Some engagement hooks but manageable |
| 0.31–0.50 | Up to 60 min | Moderate manipulation. Needs parental awareness |
| 0.51–0.70 | Up to 30 min | High manipulation. Active session management recommended |
| 0.71–1.00 | Up to 15 min (or "not recommended for children") | Very high manipulation. Consider whether the benefits justify any engagement |

### Step 4: Apply Benefit Modifier

If BDS ≥ 0.60 (game has substantial developmental value), the base recommendation can be extended by one tier (e.g., 30 min → 60 min), UNLESS RIS > 0.70 (very high risk overrides benefit extension).

If BDS < 0.20 (game has minimal developmental value) AND RIS > 0.30, the recommendation drops one tier.

### Step 5: Age Adjustment

| Child's age | Modifier |
|------------|----------|
| Under 6 | Halve the recommendation. Cap at 30 min regardless |
| 6–9 | Apply recommendation as-is |
| 10–12 | Recommendation as-is. Note where parental co-play is recommended vs. independent |
| 13–17 | Extend by one tier if content-appropriate. Teens need autonomy with guardrails |

### Step 6: Context Notes

The time recommendation always includes a brief text explanation. These are templated but customized per game:

- **Low risk, high benefit:** "This game rewards curiosity and skill. Extended sessions are fine — just ensure they don't replace physical activity and social time."
- **Low risk, low benefit:** "This game is harmless entertainment but doesn't offer much developmental value. Treat it like TV — fine in moderation."
- **High risk, high benefit:** "This game has genuine learning value BUT uses aggressive engagement design. Set a timer. Discuss the monetization mechanics with your child — it's a teachable moment."
- **High risk, low benefit:** "This game is designed primarily to maximize engagement time and spending. If your child plays it, keep sessions very short and discuss why the game wants them to keep playing."

## PART F: DISPLAY FRAMEWORK

### The Game Card (as shown in the mockup)

1. **Header:** Game title, developer, genre, age recommendation
2. **Time recommendation block:** The headline number + brief explanation (the green/amber/red box)
3. **Benefits profile:** Top 4-5 skills as colored pills with scores
4. **Risk profile:** 4 risk meters (Addictive design, Spending pressure, FOMO mechanics, Social pressure)
5. **Cost transparency:** Base price + estimated ongoing cost
6. **Last updated:** Timestamp for freshness

### Comparison View

Parents should be able to compare two games side-by-side. "My kid wants to play Genshin Impact — what's a similar game with lower risk?" The compare view shows both cards with differences highlighted.

### Filter/Search View

The searchable database allows filtering by:
- Age range
- Benefit category (show me games that develop spatial awareness)
- Risk threshold (show me games with RIS under 0.30)
- Time recommendation (show me games recommended for 60+ min sessions)
- Platform
- Price (free, under $20, under $60)
- Genre

## PART G: REVIEW PROCESS

### Tier 1: Automated Screening (all games)

Using store metadata, user review NLP, community tags, and DLC/IAP data to generate a preliminary risk flag:
- Has in-app purchases? → Flag for R2 review
- User reviews mention "addictive", "pay to win", "money grab"? → Flag for R1 review
- Has daily login rewards? → Auto-score R1.2 ≥ 1
- Free-to-play with no IAP cap? → Auto-score R2.1 = 3
- ESRB "In-Game Purchases (Includes Random Items)"? → Auto-score R1.1 ≥ 2

Estimated cost: $0.10–$1.00 per game. Coverage: 100,000+ games.

### Tier 2: Structured Community Review (top 10,000–30,000 games)

Trained volunteer or paid reviewers complete Parts A–D using the rubric. Two independent reviewers per game, with automated consistency checking. Disagreements resolved by a third reviewer.

Estimated time per review: 90 minutes for a reviewer familiar with the game, 3–4 hours for a game played specifically for review.

Estimated cost: $15–50 per review (community volunteers with QA) to $80–200 (paid reviewers).

### Tier 3: Expert Deep Review (top 2,000–5,000 games)

Clinical psychologist or game design specialist reviews with additional analysis:
- Detailed monetization audit (actual spending simulation)
- Session recording and engagement loop mapping
- Comparison to similar games
- Written narrative for "What parents need to know"

Estimated time: 4–8 hours per game.
Estimated cost: $300–800 per review.

## PART H: VALIDATION PLAN

### Internal Validation

- **Inter-rater reliability:** Target ICC ≥ 0.80 (matching SHARP-G's 0.88 ICC)
- **Consistency testing:** Rate 50 well-known games across the spectrum. Do results match expert intuition? Do parents find the recommendations useful?
- **Edge case testing:** Games that are high-benefit AND high-risk (e.g., Genshin Impact — beautiful world, genuine exploration value, but aggressive gacha). Does the rubric handle these nuances or flatten them?

### External Validation

- **Parent testing:** Do 100 parents find the time recommendations reasonable and actionable? Do they match their lived experience?
- **Academic review:** Submit the rubric for peer review. Seek endorsement from SHARP-G authors (Saini & Hodgins) and developmental psychologists
- **Developer feedback:** Share rubric with ethical game developers. Does it accurately distinguish their games from exploitative competitors?

### Ongoing Calibration

- Track whether the time recommendations correlate with real-world usage patterns (if parents share data)
- Monitor user feedback for games where the recommendation feels wrong
- Quarterly rubric review to account for new mechanics (AI-generated content, new monetization models, etc.)

## APPENDIX: Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Legend of Zelda: Tears of the Kingdom

**Benefits Profile:**
- B1.1 Problem solving: 5 (core mechanic — physics-based puzzles throughout)
- B1.2 Spatial awareness: 5 (3D world navigation, building in 3D space)
- B1.3 Strategic thinking: 3 (resource management, combat planning)
- B1.4 Critical thinking: 3 (environmental puzzles require observation)
- B1.5 Memory and attention: 3 (remembering locations, patterns)
- B1.6 Creativity: 5 (Ultrahand building system is radically open-ended)
- B1.7 Reading: 2 (some dialogue, quest descriptions)
- B1.8 Math/systems: 2 (cooking system, durability management)
- B1.9 Learning transfer: 2 (basic physics concepts)
- B1.10 Adaptive challenge: 4 (open-world allows self-directed difficulty)
- **B1 Total: 34/50 = 68%**

- B2.1 Teamwork: 0 (single-player)
- B2.2 Communication: 0
- B2.3 Empathy: 3 (caring about NPCs, helping villagers)
- B2.4 Emotional regulation: 4 (persistence through challenging shrines)
- B2.5 Ethical reasoning: 2 (minor quest choices)
- B2.6 Positive social interaction: 0 (single-player)
- **B2 Total: 9/30 = 30%**

- B3.1 Hand-eye coordination: 4 (combat requires timing)
- B3.2 Fine motor: 3 (building, aiming)
- B3.3 Reaction time: 3 (combat)
- B3.4 Physical activity: 0 (standard controller)
- **B3 Total: 10/20 = 50%**

**BDS = (0.68 × 0.50) + (0.30 × 0.30) + (0.50 × 0.20) = 0.34 + 0.09 + 0.10 = 0.53**

**Risk Profile:**
- R1: 3/30 (no variable rewards, no streaks, natural pause points, no notifications) = 0.10
- R2: 0/24 (one-time purchase, no IAP, no ads) = 0.00
- R3: 0/18 (single-player, no stranger interaction, no social pressure) = 0.00

**RIS = (0.10 × 0.45) + (0.00 × 0.30) + (0.00 × 0.25) = 0.045**

**Time recommendation:** RIS 0.045 → Base "Up to 120 min." BDS 0.53 is below the 0.60 extension threshold, so no modification. For ages 6-9, apply as-is.

**Result: "Up to 90 min" (conservatively rounded down from 120 for practical parenting guidance). Low dopamine manipulation. Natural stopping points between shrines. Rewards exploration, not urgency.**

**Benefits Profile:**
- B1.1 Problem solving: 3 (environmental puzzles, moderate)

- R1.9 Escalating commitment: 3 (character investment, account value)
- R1.10 Variable reward frequency: 3 (precisely calibrated gacha rates)
- **R1 Total: 24/30 = 0.80**

- R2.1 Spending ceiling: 3 (unlimited spending possible)
- R2.2 Pay-to-win: 2 (stronger characters from gacha)
- R2.3 Currency obfuscation: 3 (Primogems → Intertwined Fates → Wishes)
- R2.4 Spending prompts: 2 (banner countdowns, "guaranteed in X pulls")
- R2.5 Child-targeting: 1 (anime aesthetic appeals to teens)
- R2.6 Ad pressure: 0 (no ads)
- R2.7 Subscription pressure: 2 (Welkin Moon, Battle Pass)
- R2.8 Social spending pressure: 2 (character showcases, damage comparisons)
- **R2 Total: 15/24 = 0.625**

- R3.1 Social obligation: 1 (co-op is optional)
- R3.2 Competitive toxicity: 1 (Spiral Abyss creates comparison)
- R3.3 Stranger interaction: 1 (co-op with randoms)
- R3.4 Social comparison: 2 (damage showcases, character collections)
- R3.5 Identity/self-worth: 2 (account/character collection as identity)
- R3.6 Privacy: 1 (account required, standard data collection)
- **R3 Total: 8/18 = 0.44**

**RIS = (0.80 × 0.45) + (0.625 × 0.30) + (0.44 × 0.25) = 0.36 + 0.19 + 0.11 = 0.66**

**Time recommendation:** RIS 0.66 → Base "Up to 30 min." BDS 0.42 is below 0.60 so no benefit extension. RIS is below 0.70 so no override.

**Result: "30 min max." Gacha system with variable rewards. Daily login streaks. Time-limited banners create urgency. Beautiful world offsets risk — but the monetization system is designed to be compulsive.**

### Example 3: Minecraft (Survival/Creative on console — no marketplace)

**Benefits:** B1 = 38/50 (76%), B2 = 16/30 (53%), B3 = 6/20 (30%)
**BDS = (0.76 × 0.50) + (0.53 × 0.30) + (0.30 × 0.20) = 0.38 + 0.16 + 0.06 = 0.60**

**Risk:** R1 = 4/30 (0.13), R2 = 2/24 (0.08), R3 = 4/18 (0.22)
**RIS = (0.13 × 0.45) + (0.08 × 0.30) + (0.22 × 0.25) = 0.06 + 0.02 + 0.06 = 0.14**

**Time recommendation:** RIS 0.14 → Base "Up to 120 min." BDS 0.60 hits the benefit extension threshold → Could extend, but already at maximum. Apply as "Up to 120 min."

**Result: "Up to 2 hours." Exceptional developmental value across problem solving, creativity, spatial awareness, and teamwork (in multiplayer). Minimal manipulation design. Set stopping points if your child doesn't self-regulate.**

Note: Minecraft Bedrock with Marketplace would score differently on R2 due to IAP/marketplace mechanics. **This is why platform/version matters and why ratings need to be specific.**
